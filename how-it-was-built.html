Build Story

How This System Was Built

This page explains how the CM briefing moved from raw capture to a readable decision surface. The work started as a tracker, became a dashboard, widened into research, and ended as the tracked-window-first site now linked from the homepage. Just as important, the analytical lens kept getting tighter: the final version centers what was directly observed, treats source ownership and opening totals cautiously, and records why the gap-linked bursts were re-read as local capture outages rather than as proof of a new source-side event. AI tools mainly Claude Opus and Codex were used to accelerate restructuring, synthesis and implementation, but the direction stayed anchored in the prompt trail and the evidence collected along the way.

The Build Sequence

Step 1

Tracker

The first layer was a live capture system that logged vote totals, percentage shifts, deltas, gaps and alerts into the raw CSV history. That established a reproducible record instead of relying on impressions from one moment on the source page.

Step 2

Dashboard

The second layer turned the capture stream into a readable dashboard. It surfaced movement, spikes, gap-linked jumps and alerts so the recorded window could be seen as a developing story rather than as isolated numbers.

Step 3

Research

The third layer added public-role context, candidate track records, wider INC context and caution around mismatch causes. That step made it possible to interpret the poll without pretending the tracker alone answered every political question.

Step 4

Final Site

The last layer reorganised everything into a first-visit briefing: homepage, candidate profiles, INC implications page and this build-history page. The result is a single surface that keeps the tracked window primary while still showing the research and the caveats around it.

Evidence chain: tracker data created the record, the dashboard exposed patterns inside that record, research gave those patterns political context, and the final site arranged the material so a newcomer could use it to think about the next CM question without starting from rumours.

Source under audit

thenextcm.com was the public page being tracked. It was not treated as an official source, and we could not establish who owned it or when it was launched. That is why the system was built around capture and audit rather than around trust: the goal was to preserve what the page showed over time, compare spikes and gaps against the recorded context, and separate observed movement from later interpretation.

Making the source link obvious on the homepage matters for the same reason. The reader should be able to inspect the exact page under discussion, see that it is an unofficial poll surface, and then decide how much weight to place on the analysis built on top of that record.

How the read was tightened

Tracked window became primary

As the site matured, the strongest claims were limited to the tracked window. The first valid snapshot already carried a substantial opening base, so the final briefing does not pretend the tracker saw the poll from zero or knows how that opening base was formed.

Gap rows were rechecked against capture conditions

Some large rows initially looked like they might reflect source-side batching. That explanation was later narrowed. The better fit was local connectivity gaps during capture: once those post-gap totals are normalized for elapsed gap time, they closely match the surrounding vote rates instead of behaving like a separate surge.

Observed facts stayed separate from inference

The final briefing keeps a hard line between what the record clearly shows and what remains unknown. Satheesan's dominance inside the captured window is an observed result; the exact cause of the opening base, the site's intent, and any hidden intervention remain interpretations rather than proof.

Why this matters: the build did not just add more pages. It also removed weaker explanations, tightened claims to the evidence, and made that audit trail visible to a first-time reader.

What Each Layer Added

From tracker to dashboard

The system stopped being just a log file and became a surface that could show spikes, capture shares, hourly changes and alert rows.

From dashboard to research

The numbers were no longer treated as self-explanatory. Candidate roles, track records and INC context were added so the briefing could explain what a selection might mean, not just who moved where.

From research to site

The final site reorganised the material for decision-making: start with tracked-window evidence, move to the candidate pages for depth, then use the INC implications page for the wider political read.

AI role: Claude Opus and Codex helped accelerate implementation, iteration and restructuring. They did not replace the need to choose the analytical lens, check the mismatches in the data, or decide how the story should be told.
