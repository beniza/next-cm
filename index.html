Kerala CM 2026 Poll Audit And Candidate Briefing

This site begins with an unofficial Kerala CM poll hosted at thenextcm.com. We could not establish who owns thenextcm.com or when it was launched, so the source itself has to be treated cautiously. Our experiment was simple: track the public-facing vote stream over time, audit whether it looked obviously dumped or more continuously updated, and then ask what the captured record actually showed, including what happened across the local connectivity gaps during capture.

What follows is a disciplined read of the record we captured. Within the tracked window, the visible stream looked fairly consistent rather than crudely dumped, but hidden manipulation still cannot be ruled out. The strongest result inside that window was clear: Satheesan received about 90% of all newly observed votes, while Venugopal and Chennithala stayed near 5% each. The larger post-gap jumps are best explained by local connectivity gaps during capture, and once normalized for elapsed gap time they closely match the surrounding vote rates.

Read the audit first

The homepage is organized around what was observed in the tracked stream, not around assumptions about who built the source or what the source intended to do.

Separate what was seen from what was inferred

The stream tells us that Satheesan dominated newly arriving votes. It also tells us that the post-gap recovery rows fit offline catch-up better than a fresh anomaly, but it does not tell us, by itself, why Venugopal started with a large opening base or what was happening behind the source page.

Use the rest of the site as separate layers

The candidate pages add track record and public-role context, the charts visualize what the tracker captured, and the INC implications page explores what each scenario could mean if the observed pattern were taken seriously.

Methodology & Caveats

This page treats the tracked window as the primary evidence base. That means the strongest claims come from what changed between the first and last valid snapshots, while anything about the source itself, the reason for the opening base, or the difference between tracked flow and full totals remains a cautious interpretation rather than a certainty. The key verified point is that the largest post-gap rows fit local offline capture outages more closely than they fit a sudden new change in the source stream.

Research Context

Candidate roles

Venugopal is the AICC organisation general secretary and an MP.[1] Satheesan led the opposition in Kerala before the 2026 win.[2] Chennithala brings CWC, KPCC and ministerial experience.[3]

INC wider context

The party won Himachal in 2022, Karnataka in 2023, Telangana in 2023, and improved to 99 Lok Sabha seats in 2024, which gives the Kerala choice importance beyond the state itself.[4][5][6][7]

What remains unresolved

This homepage answers what the tracker showed, but Venugopal's opening base remains an unresolved mystery. The separate INC implications page moves beyond the audit and asks what each selection would mean for the party's opportunities and challenges.

Neutrality rule: this site describes tracked data and publicly available context. It does not endorse any candidate, and it does not claim to have proved whether the source poll was clean, manipulated, or something in between.
